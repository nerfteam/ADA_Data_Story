The visual representatinon of the age analysis with added gender dimensionality for climate change quotes (i.e. the second plot), as well as the mean values, are similar - we could ask ourselves if these two distributions are coming from a same underlying distribution. Since the std values are similar, we can apply the ttest.

After getting a p-value = 1.79e-05 (which is less than ordinary 0.01), we can conclude that the difference in the mean values is significant, therefore these two distributions most likely come from two different distributions - where the mean for females is a bit lower than the one for males.

This could lead us to the conclusion that older female politicians talk less about climate change than their male colleagues. However, taking the first plot into the account, we can observe a much bigger number of older male politicians than female - making the older female politicians, to some extent, underrepresented.

Linear Regression

Some more analysis...



Okay, that's enough of analysing the isolated parameters. We have observed some interesting statistics, but is it relevant to our problem?
Recall that our analysis should be based on different political and demographic parameters of speakers. So, let's see which of aforementioned parameters really matter.
As Bob West would say, 'Linear regression gives us free p-values'. So why would not we utilize this present.

The main goal of the linear regression is to determine the impact given parameters have on the number of climate change quotes (i.e the outcome is the number of climate change related quotes).
When it comes to features, we will feed the linear regression with following ones: degree_num, age, party_name, gender_name, was_presidental_candidate, was_in_congres, party_count, was_president, numOccurrences, totalNumOccurrences, total_num_quotes.

Based on the results of linear regression we can conclude:
  • Features age and gender have p-values bigger than 0.05, and therefore do not have significant influence on outcome, i.e. on the number of quotations regarding climate change.
  • Political features (was_candidate, was_in_congres, was_president, party_count, party_name) have significant impact on outcome.
  • Number of degrees have significant impact on outcome.
  • Features numOccurrences, totalNumOccurrences, total_num_quotes, which are more media related, have significant impact on outcome.

    • If you have been reading carefully (don't worry we won't judge if you haven't) you certainly remember that during gender analysis we observed that women talk more about climate change than men, relatively speaking (comparing climate_change_quotes/all_quotes ratios). Surprisingly, linear regression is not gender biased - the gender feature is irrelevant. This must mean that another feature is the influential one, and that the gender feature is just masking it.

    We have a strong hunch that this mystical feature is none other than the party name (which indeed is a significant feature).
